Phase portraits — the reference joint's limit cycle

Each panel plots one leg joint against itself: its angle θ along the horizontal, the velocity θ̇ it carried at that angle along the vertical, traced over the steady-cruise window (the first 2 s of acceleration dropped). A stride is one lap around the loop, so a whole episode of running is drawn on top of itself — and how tightly the laps stack is the measurement. Read it this way: a tight closed band is a clean limit-cycle gait, every stride retracing the last; a diffuse cloud is limb motion that only incidentally moves the torso. All four panels share one θ/θ̇ range, so the cycles are comparable in size. The dashed line is the Poincaré section (θ crossing its own mean, upward); dots mark the crossings.

Poincaré return maps — does the cycle come back to the same place?

Each lap of the phase portrait punches through the Poincaré section once, carrying some velocity θ̇ as it goes. This plot keeps only those punctures and throws the rest of the trajectory away: every dot pairs the velocity at one crossing (horizontal) with the velocity at the very next one (vertical), so a dot answers the question given this stride, what did the stride after it do? Both axes carry the same quantity over one shared range, which puts the diagonal — a stride repeating its predecessor exactly — in the same place in every panel. Read it this way: a stable cycle pierces the section at one point, so its dots cluster tight on the diagonal; spread along the diagonal means the gait is periodic but drifts in speed, and scatter away from it is stride-to-stride variability.

Metric

The math behind each metric

Every number above, defined. The task reward reads one scalar — forward torso speed — with no control or energy term and no dependence on how the speed is produced, so two policies at equal return can inject very different energy and can reach that speed with a real gait or with motion that is only incidentally forward. These two families measure what return cannot see. All of them read the MuJoCo physics directly, so any saved policy is re-rolled and scored without retraining.

Energy — what the gait cost

Actuator work feeds E⁺, CoT, return/joule

τj = gearj · uj
Wj = τj · Δqj
E⁺ = Σsteps Σj max(Wj, 0)

The six leg motors are plain fixed-gain torque actuators, so the torque on joint j is its gear times the commanded action — exactly, and held constant across a control step under the zero-order hold. The work it does over that step is therefore torque × angle change, exact with no sub-step integration. Summing only the positive part over all six joints and every step gives the gross energy the actuators delivered; these motors recover nothing from braking, so negative work is not a credit.

Cost of transport bar · lower is better

CoT = E⁺ ⁄ (M · g · d)   M = 14 kg

Energy per unit weight per unit distance, with d the forward root-x travel — dimensionless, and the headline efficiency number. Dividing by distance normalizes out the fact that a faster policy covers more ground on the same clock. Read it as: how many joules per kilogram-metre does this gait cost? The absolute value sits far above an animal's — raw positive work, a 2-D model, no regeneration, a speed-only reward — so it is read across policies, never against biology.

Return per joule bar · higher is better

R ⁄ E⁺

The task reward bought per joule injected. Where cost of transport asks the price of distance, this asks the price of reward — the two can disagree, because return saturates at 10 m/s while the energy bill does not. Read it as efficiency in the task's own currency.

Spectral scan — finding the gait (whole body: all six joints + both feet)

How the curve is made underlies every number in this group

Take one joint's angle over the cruise window — a squiggle in time. Any squiggle can be rebuilt by adding together sine waves, and the power spectrum P(f) is the recipe: how much of the wiggle happens at each rate. A joint swinging cleanly three times a second gives a tall spike at 3 Hz and little else; a joint trembling randomly gives a low flat smear across every rate. Every number in this group is one question asked of that curve.

x̃(t) = x(t) − (a·t + b)
P(f) = Welch{ x̃(t) }  (Hann, 8 half-overlapping segments)
band = [0.5, 8] Hz  (capped at 0.45·fs)

Control intervals are irregular, so each signal is first interpolated onto a uniform grid at fs = 1/median(Δt). Detrending then subtracts the best-fit line, killing the slow posture creep that would otherwise swamp the spectrum and every phase measure downstream. Welch's method chops the signal into eight half-overlapping chunks and averages their FFTs: a single FFT of a real signal stays noisy however long you record, because extra data buys more frequency bins rather than a cleaner estimate in each one — and the Hann taper stops each chunk's hard edges from smearing a fake jump across all frequencies. Reading only inside the stride band is what stops a standing policy from scoring: a motionless limb's power all sits at DC (f = 0), the constant offset that says where the limb rests rather than that it moves.

Three numbers this produces — all supporting, none of them ranking
β = Σf ∈ band P(f) ⁄ Σf > 0 P(f)

Of the motion that exists, how much is stride-rate motion? DC is kept out of the denominator for the reason above. This is the gate's evidence that a gait exists at all, built to catch a policy that stands and jitters — so it ranks nothing here, which is the intended outcome. All four: 0.98, spread 0.006.

f0 = argmaxf ∈ band P(f)  ·  stride freq = median over the 8 signals

Where is the tallest spike? That is the stride rate — 3 Hz means three strides a second — taken as the median across the eight signals so one odd joint cannot drag it. Purely descriptive: 3.68 Hz and 3.00 Hz are both perfectly good gaits. Range 3.00–3.68 Hz.

pk = P(fk) ⁄ Σ P  ·  H = −Σk pk · log pk ⁄ log N

How spread out is the whole curve? Treat the in-band spectrum as a probability distribution over frequency and take its Shannon entropy, normalized by log N over the N in-band bins: 0 is a pure tone, 1 an even smear. Kept as an honest record of a measure that was tried and did not work — entropy counts every bin, and a real gait has a long tail of small-but-nonzero bins that pin it at a middling value however clean the fundamental is. 0.696–0.751, spread 0.055.

Fundamental power fraction ranks bar + table · higher is better

δ = max(0.15·f0, 1.5·Δf)
ρpeak = Σ|f − f0| ≤ δ P(f)  ⁄  Σf ∈ band P(f)

How much of the in-band power is concentrated at that one spike? Near 1 means the motion is essentially a single clean sine wave; lower means the power is smeared across many frequencies — a messier wiggle. This is the one number in the group that separates the policies. 0.73 → 0.86, spread 0.129.

Two details look arbitrary and are not. δ sets how wide "at the spike" counts: a windowed sine never gives an infinitely thin spike, because the Hann taper spreads it into a main lobe a couple of bins wide (Δf = bin width). The 1.5·Δf floor guarantees δ is never narrower than that lobe, which would slice off part of the peak and score even a perfect tone as impure. And harmonics are excluded: a real gait is periodic without being sinusoidal, so it puts energy at f0 and also at 2f0, 3f0, … Counting those would let a 3 Hz fundamental's harmonics fill the whole 0.5–8 Hz band and saturate the measure near 1 for every policy.

It carries a second job: the joint with the largest ρpeak is the cleanest oscillator in the body, so it is the one the Poincaré section gets built on — which is what the reference-joint group below measures against.

Reference joint — how good is the gait? (these move with the joint selector)

Periodicity — autocorrelation peak bar · higher is better

ρ(τ) = Σt θ̃(t)·θ̃(t+τ)  ⁄  Σt θ̃(t)²
periodicity = maxτ ρ(τ),  τ ∈ [1/fmax, 1/fmin]

The normalized autocorrelation asks how much of the waveform comes back after a lag τ; the score is the best lag inside the stride band. A signal that repeats exactly returns to 1. Read it as the strength of the single best repeat period — it is the phase portrait's band tightness expressed as one number.

Stride period and its CV bar · lower is better

θa(t) = θ̃(t) + i·ℋ{θ̃}(t)  (FFT Hilbert)
φ(t) = unwrap arg θa(t)
tk : φ(tk) = 2πk,  Tk = tk+1 − tk
CV = std(Tk) ⁄ mean(Tk)

The analytic signal gives an instantaneous phase; one stride is one 2π of it, and the boundaries tk are found by interpolating that phase. The coefficient of variation of the resulting durations is 0 when every stride takes exactly the same time. Phase-based segmentation counts one cycle per 2π regardless of waveform shape, where a threshold-crossing counter would double-count a multi-lobed stride.

Poincaré dispersion bar · lower is better

Σ : θ̃ = 0, ascending  (the section)
sk = (θ1..6, θ̇1..6) ∈ ℝ¹²  at crossing k
dP = (1/K) · Σk ‖ (sk − s̄) ⁄ σ ‖2

At each upward mean-crossing of the reference joint, the full twelve-dimensional leg state — all six angles and all six velocities — is interpolated to the crossing instant, standardized per coordinate by its own window std, and measured from the crossings' centroid. A stable limit cycle pierces the section at one point, so dP → 0; a diffuse cloud is quasi-random limb motion that happens to move the torso. Standardizing stops fast joints drowning slow ones, and taking the joint state of all six limbs per crossing of one reference is what makes this a whole-gait measure rather than a one-joint one — it is the return map's scatter, generalized from 2 dimensions to 12.

Front/back phase-locking bar · higher is better

PLV = | (1/T) · Σt e i(φb(t) − φf(t)) |

With φb, φf the analytic phases of the back and front thigh, this is 1 exactly when the inter-limb phase difference is constant — any fixed offset, as in a bound or gallop — and small when the two limbs wander independently. It is invariant to which relative phase is held, so it scores coordination without presupposing a particular gait. Being a back-vs-front comparison, it is the same number whichever reference joint you select.

The gate — when the numbers are allowed to exist

Gait detection suppresses the four measures above

gait_detected = (n_strides ≥ 3) ∧ (mean β > 0.2)

A near-stationary policy's low-amplitude jitter still has an analytic phase, so the Hilbert segmentation would happily manufacture "strides" out of noise and then score them. Periodicity, stride CV, Poincaré dispersion and PLV are therefore reported only when a gait is actually present: at least three stride cycles and a real fraction of the limb-motion power inside the stride band. When the gate fails, those four come back as NaN — shown as — — and only β and the stride count survive, so the absence of a gait stays visible rather than being scored as a bad one.
